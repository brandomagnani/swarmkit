Ten years later, Google faced a biometric data lawsuit (2025) and subsequent settlement, suggesting the core concerns were both legitimate and prescient.

📅 What Happened

The 2015 complaint focused on Chrome Sync being enabled by default on school Chromebooks, allowing Google to track browsing data. Google promised to disable data sharing between Sync and other services, but this was a minor concession. Over the following decade: (1) The 2015 complaint did not result in a major FTC settlement at the time, indicating regulatory action was slow; (2) Google maintained it didn't use student data for advertising, but the complaint alleged data was being 'mined for non-advertising purposes'; (3) By 2025, Google faced a new lawsuit alleging collection of biometric data (voiceprints, facial templates) from students through G Suite without parental consent; (4) Google settled the 2025 biometric lawsuit for $8.75 million, finally acknowledging violations; (5) The broader issue remained unresolved: Google collected more student data than strictly necessary, and schools had limited ability to prevent it due to network effects and locked-in deployments. The EFF's core complaint proved prescient—Google was indeed collecting data beyond legitimate educational purposes, and this behavior continued largely unabated for a decade until biometric collection became the focal point.

Commenters discussed the historical evolution of these techniques, their use in game copy protection (Elite), the Skype case study, and the limitations of disassemblers like IDA Pro. The conversation revealed the ongoing arms race between malware authors and security analysts.

📅 What Happened

Over the 10 years since this article (2015-2025), anti-disassembly techniques evolved significantly. The major developments were: (1) Ghidra, the NSA's open-source reverse engineering tool, was released in March 2019, providing a free alternative to expensive IDA Pro with comparable decompilation capabilities; (2) IDA Pro improved but remained expensive and had known limitations with stripped binaries; (3) Malware authors escalated sophistication, with modern packers using 100+ layers of encryption (versus ~50 in 2015); (4) AI/ML-assisted decompilation tools emerged; (5) StarForce DRM largely faded from gaming due to industry shift to digital distribution (Steam); (6) Skype obfuscation became less relevant after Microsoft's acquisition and transition to Skype for Business; (7) Code obfuscation became increasingly commoditized through tools like PELock, which was founded in 2015 and continued active development.

This proved highly prescient. By 2025, automated decompilation tools (Ghidra, Hex-Rays decompiler, AI-assisted tools) became standard practice. The user correctly identified that manual analysis wouldn't scale with increasing packer complexity, and that automation was the inevitable solution. The arms race observation remains apt—modern malware still uses sophisticated packing, but automation counters it.

📅 What Happened

WhatsApp, owned by Facebook (acquired for $19B in 2014), deliberately blocked Telegram links by hardcoding 'telegram' as a 'bad host' in their Android app. This became a major story about anticompetitive behavior. Over the next decade: WhatsApp grew to 3 billion users and expanded to become Meta's messaging hub alongside Messenger, integrated with business features and monetized through ads in Stories (not private chats). Telegram grew to 1 billion users as a feature-rich alternative with superior UX (desktop client, cloud sync, bots). Signal remained small (~70 million users) despite being cryptographically superior, hampered by multi-device limitations and niche appeal. Facebook faced antitrust scrutiny (EU DMA, various regulators) but maintained dominance. The block eventually became irrelevant as WhatsApp evolved into something Telegram couldn't displace.
